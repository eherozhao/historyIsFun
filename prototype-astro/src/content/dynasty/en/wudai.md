In 907, the warlord Zhu Wen forced the last Tang emperor from his throne and proclaimed the Later Liang dynasty — ending nearly three centuries of Tang rule and plunging China into its most turbulent half-century since the fall of the Han. Over the next fifty-three years, five short-lived dynasties — Later Liang, Later Tang, Later Jin, Later Han, and Later Zhou — rose and fell in the north, while ten independent kingdoms carved out domains across the south and in Shanxi.

Yet this age of fragmentation was also one of profound transformation. War in the north drove millions southward, accelerating the economic shift from the Yellow River basin to the Yangtze delta. Southern Tang, Wuyue, and other southern states fostered commerce, art, and literature in relative peace. The ci lyric matured from popular song into a major literary form. And in the final decade, Emperor Shizong of Later Zhou launched reforms so effective that his general Zhao Kuangyin — who seized the throne in the Chenqiao coup of 960 — could build upon them to reunify China and found the Song dynasty.
