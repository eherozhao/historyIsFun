  economy:
    - title: Iron Tools and Ox-Drawn Ploughing
      description: Iron agricultural implements spread from the late Spring and Autumn period; ox-drawn ploughing became widespread during the Warring States. These innovations dramatically increased agricultural productivity and drove the privatisation of land.
    - title: Dujiangyan and Zhengguo Canal
      description: Li Bing and his son built the Dujiangyan irrigation system (256 BCE), transforming the Chengdu Plain into the "Land of Abundance." The Zhengguo Canal irrigated the Guanzhong Plain. These projects demonstrate the engineering sophistication of the Warring States.
  politics:
    - title: From Enfeoffment to Commanderies
      description: Counties appeared within states during the Spring and Autumn period; the commandery-county system gradually replaced feudal enfeoffment during the Warring States. This transition from aristocratic to bureaucratic governance laid the groundwork for Qin's centralised empire.
    - title: Reform Movements
      description: States competed through reform — Li Kui in Wei, Wu Qi in Chu, Shang Yang in Qin. These reforms abolished aristocratic privilege, rewarded military merit, and promoted rule by law, fundamentally reshaping China's political structure.
  culture:
    - title: Hundred Schools of Thought
      description: Confucians championed benevolence and ritual; Daoists exalted nature and non-action; Mohists preached universal love and opposition to war; Legalists promoted law and statecraft. This intellectual ferment constituted the golden age of Chinese philosophy.
    - title: Chu Ci and the Literary Tradition
      description: Qu Yuan's *Li Sao* founded the Chinese romantic literary tradition. Paired with the *Book of Songs*, the two are called "Feng and Sao" — the twin wellsprings of Chinese literature. The Chu Ci's vivid imagination and intense personal emotion have inspired writers ever since.

The Eastern Zhou divides into the Spring and Autumn (770–476 BCE) and Warring States (475–221 BCE) periods — the most turbulent era of social transformation in Chinese history. After the Zhou king's authority collapsed, rival lords vied for hegemony and states waged wars of annexation, dismantling the old feudal order and forging new political institutions in the crucible of conflict.

Yet political turmoil proved the catalyst for intellectual brilliance. The Hundred Schools of Thought represent the most free and creative epoch in Chinese intellectual history — Confucius's benevolence, Laozi's non-action, Mozi's universal love, Han Fei's rule of law remain keys to understanding Chinese culture today. The spread of iron tools and ox-drawn ploughs, and monumental hydraulic projects like Dujiangyan, drove profound material transformation. Over five centuries of division and competition culminated in Qin's unification of the six states.
