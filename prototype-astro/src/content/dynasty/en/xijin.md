The Western Jin was a brief reunification after the Three Kingdoms. Sima Yan usurped Wei and conquered Wu in 280, but unity lasted barely a decade. His mass enfeoffment of Sima princes triggered the sixteen-year War of the Eight Princes, which exhausted the empire and opened the door to nomadic invasion.

The Disaster of Yongjia in 311 saw Luoyang fall; Chang'an followed in 316. Millions of northern Chinese fled south in the "Crossing South of the Gentry," and China entered a prolonged north-south division. Though short-lived, the Western Jin's legacy — the crystallisation of gate-clan politics and the spiritual heritage of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove — profoundly shaped the centuries that followed.
