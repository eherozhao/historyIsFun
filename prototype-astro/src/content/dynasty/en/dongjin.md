The Eastern Jin represents the high-water mark of aristocratic politics in Chinese history. After the Western Jin fell, émigré gentry established a regime at Jiankang characterised by shared governance between the throne and the great clans. The Wang and Xie families took turns at the helm — "the Wang and Sima families share the realm" captures the era's political reality.

Yet it was precisely under this arrangement of partial retreat that Jiangnan culture flourished as never before. Wang Xizhi's calligraphy, Tao Yuanming's pastoral poetry, and Xie Lingyun's landscape verse rank among the supreme achievements of Chinese culture. The victory at Fei River in 383 ensured the survival and continued development of southern civilisation. In 420 Liu Yu usurped the Jin and founded the Song, ending the Eastern Jin and ushering in the Northern and Southern Dynasties.
