achievements:
  economy:
    - title: Unified Currency and Weights
      description: The round copper coin with a square hole — the ban liang — replaced the six incompatible currency systems of the former states. Standardised measures of weight, volume, and length were enforced across the empire. This was the first national economic standardisation in Chinese history and the foundation of every subsequent dynasty's fiscal system.
      artifact: Qin ban liang coin
      museum: National Museum of China, Beijing
    - title: Imperial Road Network and Lingqu Canal
      description: Over 6,000 kilometres of imperial highways (*chidao*) radiated from Xianyang, with a reserved central lane for imperial use. The Lingqu Canal, completed in 214 BCE, connected the Xiang and Li rivers, enabling grain shipment by water from the Central Plains to the newly conquered south — the logistical key to holding the Lingnan region.
  politics:
    - title: Commandery-County System
      description: Abolishing feudal lordship entirely, the empire was divided into thirty-six commanderies (later forty-plus), each subdivided into counties administered by royally appointed officials who could be promoted, transferred, or dismissed. This system severed the hereditary roots of regional separatism and became the template for local governance across every subsequent Chinese dynasty for two thousand years.
    - title: Three Excellencies and Nine Ministers
      description: The central government was organised around three senior officials — Chancellor (administration), Grand Commandant (military), and Imperial Counsellor (oversight) — with nine specialised ministries below them. This bureaucratic architecture, centred on the emperor and staffed by appointed officials rather than hereditary nobles, was adopted wholesale by the Han and refined over centuries.
  culture:
    - title: Terracotta Army
      description: Discovered in 1974 by farmers digging a well east of the First Emperor's mausoleum, the terracotta pits contain over 8,000 life-sized clay soldiers, horses, and chariots — each with individualised facial features — arrayed in battle formation to guard the emperor in death. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most significant archaeological discoveries of the twentieth century.
      artifact: Terracotta Warriors and Horses
      museum: Museum of the Terracotta Warriors (Qin Shi Huang Mausoleum), Xi'an
    - title: Small-Seal Script and Stele Inscriptions
      description: Li Si standardised the small-seal script (*xiaozhuan*) as the empire's official writing system and composed the texts for steles erected at Taishan, Langya, and other sacred peaks, proclaiming the First Emperor's achievement in standardised language. These steles are the oldest surviving official Qin texts and the primary specimens of xiaozhuan calligraphy.
      artifact: Langya Stele (rubbing)
      museum: National Museum of China, Beijing

In 221 BCE, after a decade of systematic conquest, the King of Qin completed the unification of all six rival states and invented an entirely new title for himself: *huangdi*, First Emperor. The scale of what followed was without precedent. Feudal lordship was abolished across the empire and replaced with directly administered commanderies. Script, coinage, weights and measures, and even the axle width of carts were standardised from the Yellow River to the South China Sea. A network of imperial highways radiated from the capital Xianyang; a canal connected the Yangtze basin to the far south; and three hundred thousand soldiers and labourers constructed a northern frontier wall against the Xiongnu.

The Qin Dynasty lasted fifteen years. Brutal corvée labour, crushing taxation, and the psychological shock of a world remade overnight drove successive rebellions. The First Emperor died on his fifth imperial tour in 210 BCE; his trusted eunuch Zhao Gao manipulated the succession and destroyed the court from within; and by 206 BCE Liu Bang's forces were at the gates of Xianyang. Yet the institutional revolution proved indestructible. The Han Dynasty that replaced Qin inherited its commandery system, its bureaucratic machinery, and its ideal of unified empire. Every subsequent dynasty did the same. Historians have noted the paradox: the most short-lived major dynasty in Chinese history became the one whose legacy lasted longest.
