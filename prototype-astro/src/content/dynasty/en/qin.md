## Unification of the Six States

In 221 BCE, after a decade of calculated warfare, the King of Qin completed the conquest of the last six rival states — Han, Zhao, Wei, Chu, Yan, and Qi — ending centuries of the Warring States period. Ying Zheng, the conquering king, coined a new title for himself: *Huangdi* (皇帝), the First August Emperor. He declared that his successors would rule as Second, Third Emperor — and so on for ten thousand generations. The dynasty lasted fourteen years.

## Administrative Revolution

Qin Shi Huang imposed standardisation at a scale never before attempted. The old feudal system of hereditary lords was abolished; China was reorganised into thirty-six commanderies (*jun*), each subdivided into counties (*xian*) administered by royally appointed officials. Writing was unified under a standardised script, ending centuries of regional variation. Weights and measures, coinage, and even the axle width of carts were brought into conformity — so that a cart's wheels could follow the ruts worn in any road across the empire.

## The Great Wall and Imperial Roads

To defend against Xiongnu raids from the steppe, the emperor conscripted hundreds of thousands of labourers to connect and extend the defensive walls built by earlier states into a continuous northern barrier — the ancestor of what became the Great Wall. Simultaneously, a vast network of imperial highways (*chidao*) radiated from the capital Xianyang, linking the former kingdoms. Tree-lined and shouldered for infantry, these roads were the arteries of the new state.

## Key Figures

- **Qin Shi Huang (Ying Zheng)** 259–210 BCE — the First Emperor; forger of China's unitary state, standardiser of script and currency, builder of the Great Wall
- **Li Si** — chief minister; architect of the administrative reforms and the script standardisation; author of the *Remonstrance Against Expelling Guests*
- **Meng Tian** — general who commanded the Great Wall construction campaign and led three hundred thousand soldiers against the Xiongnu; traditionally credited with refining the writing brush
- **Shang Yang** (pre-Qin reformer) — his earlier reforms — rewarding agriculture and military service, dismantling aristocratic privilege — laid the institutional groundwork for Qin's eventual dominance

## The Burning of Books and Burial of Scholars

To silence dissent and enforce ideological conformity, Qin Shi Huang, on Li Si's recommendation, ordered the burning of most books — keeping only texts on medicine, agriculture, and divination. In a separate episode, over four hundred scholars, accused of criticising the emperor and spreading false rumours, were executed. The loss of pre-Qin literature was incalculable; many texts survive only in quotations preserved in later compilations.

## The Terracotta Army

From early in his reign, the emperor ordered the construction of a vast mausoleum complex near Mount Li. Over thirty years, more than 700,000 conscripts and craftsmen worked on the project. In 1974, farmers digging a well east of the tomb discovered the pit containing the Terracotta Army — over 8,000 life-sized clay soldiers, horses, and chariots, each with individualised features, arrayed in battle formation to guard the emperor in the afterlife. The site is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site, often called the "Eighth Wonder of the Ancient World."

## A Brief Dynasty, an Enduring Template

The Qin Dynasty lasted barely fifteen years. Brutal taxation, forced labour, and the upheaval of unification sparked a series of rebellions. By 206 BCE the dynasty had collapsed. Yet its institutional legacy proved indestructible. The Han Dynasty that followed inherited — and refined — the commandery-county system, the emperor-bureaucracy apparatus, and the ideal of centralised imperial rule. Historians summarise it simply: *Han inherited Qin's institutions.* The basic grammar of Chinese governance, from the commandery to the civil service exam, flows from this brief, violent dynasty.
