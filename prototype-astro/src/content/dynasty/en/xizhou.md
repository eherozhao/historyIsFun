  politics:
    - title: Feudal Enfeoffment
      description: The Zhou king granted land and people to kinsmen and meritorious officials, creating vassal states. Lords owed tribute and military service. This kinship-based political network sustained Western Zhou rule for nearly three centuries.
    - title: Patrilineal Succession System

The Western Zhou was the foundational era of Chinese ritual civilisation. After King Wu conquered the Shang, the Duke of Zhou codified a comprehensive system of rites and music built on feudal enfeoffment and patrilineal succession — the king enfeoffed lords, lords served the throne, and kinship ties maintained the hierarchy. This order sustained nearly three centuries of stable rule and became the prototype of Confucius's ideal society.

The Western Zhou's cultural legacy runs deep: the earliest poems in the *Book of Songs* date from this period, and bronze inscriptions preserve invaluable historical records. In its later years royal authority declined — King Li's tyranny provoked a commoners' revolt, and King You's folly brought the Quanrong sacking of Haojing. In 771 BCE the Western Zhou fell, and King Ping's eastward move to Luoyi ushered in the transformative age of the Spring and Autumn and Warring States.
