achievements:
  economy:
    - title: The Silk Road at Its Height
      description: The Tang dynasty was the Silk Road's golden age. Chang'an served as the eastern anchor of a trade network stretching to the Mediterranean. Chinese silk, ceramics, and tea flowed west; glassware, cotton, and spices flowed east. Persian and Sogdian merchants maintained permanent quarters in Chang'an and Luoyang.
    - title: Equal-Field System and Tax Reform
      description: The Tang codified the equal-field system, allocating farmland to adult males and recovering it at death. The paired zu-yong-diao tax system — grain, labour, and cloth — replaced the chaotic Sui-era levies and underpinned the economic recovery of the early Tang, enabling the prosperity of the Zhenguan and Kaiyuan eras.
  politics:
    - title: Three Departments and Six Ministries
      description: The Tang perfected the Sui-era system of three deliberative departments (drafting, review, and execution) checked against each other, with six specialised ministries below them. This architecture became the template for subsequent Chinese dynasties and was adopted in modified form by Japan, Korea, and Vietnam.
    - title: Expansion of the Civil Examinations
      description: The Tang dramatically expanded the examination system, adding new subject areas and recruiting talent regardless of aristocratic lineage. The *jinshi* degree became the most prestigious pathway to office, gradually eroding the dominance of the great clans and creating the scholar-official class that would define elite Chinese culture.
  culture:
    - title: Tang Poetry
      description: The *Complete Tang Poems* anthologises 48,900 poems by 2,200 authors — and this is only a fraction of what was written. Li Bai, Du Fu, Wang Wei, Bai Juyi, and dozens of others explored every emotional register available to the human voice. Tang poetry remains the touchstone of Chinese literary education and the most recited verse in the language.

    - title: Tang Sancai Ceramics
      description: Tang three-colour lead-glazed earthenware — yellow, green, and white — produced some of the most vivid ceramic sculptures in Chinese history. Camels, horses, court ladies, and foreign musicians were buried with the aristocracy along the Silk Road corridors. They are among the most recognisable symbols of Tang cosmopolitan culture.

    - title: Dunhuang Cave Paintings
      description: The Mogao Caves at Dunhuang, carved from the Northern Liang through the Yuan dynasty, reached their artistic peak under the Tang. The murals synthesise Central Asian, Indian, and Chinese Buddhist iconography on an extraordinary scale. The Dunhuang manuscripts — discovered in 1900 — preserved thousands of texts lost elsewhere, including the world's oldest dated printed book.
      artifact: Cave 156 mural — Procession of Zhang Yichao
      museum: Dunhuang Academy, Gansu

In 618, as the Sui Dynasty collapsed under its own imperial overreach, the military governor Li Yuan marched on Chang'an and founded the Tang. His son Li Shimin — Emperor Taizong — seized the throne through the Xuanwu Gate Incident and inaugurated the Zhenguan era, a period of pragmatic governance, reduced taxation, and open court debate that became the benchmark against which every subsequent Chinese ruler was measured. Under Emperor Xuanzong's Kaiyuan reign (713–741), the Tang reached its zenith: Chang'an surpassed one million inhabitants and became the most cosmopolitan city on earth, the Silk Road pulsed with unprecedented traffic, and Chinese poetry entered its golden age.

The An-Shi Rebellion of 755 broke the Tang's back. Eight years of war devastated the north, killed tens of millions, and permanently weakened central authority. Regional military governors became hereditary warlords; eunuchs manipulated the succession; court factions consumed the last reserves of administrative energy. The Huang Chao Rebellion (875–884) completed the destruction, and in 907 the warlord Zhu Wen deposed the last Tang emperor. The dynasty's legacy, however, proved as durable as its walls were not — Tang institutions, poetry, ceramics, and Buddhism spread across East Asia and shaped civilisations from Japan to Vietnam for centuries.
