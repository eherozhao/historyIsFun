## Rise and Unification

In 618, as the Sui Dynasty collapsed under its own ambition, Li Yuan seized the capital Chang'an and proclaimed the Tang Dynasty. His son Li Shimin — later Emperor Taizong — seized the throne through the Xuanwu Gate Incident and inaugurated the "Reign of Zhenguan," a period of pragmatic governance, reduced taxation, and open court debate that became a model for all subsequent dynasties.

## The Height of Prosperity

Under Emperor Xuanzong's Kaiyuan era (713–741), Tang China reached its zenith. Chang'an, with a population exceeding one million, was among the largest cities in the world. Merchants, diplomats, and monks from Persia, Arabia, Silla, and Japan crowded its broad avenues. The Silk Road hummed with traffic; Buddhism, Zoroastrianism, Nestorianism, and Islam all found temples within the city walls.

## The Golden Age of Poetry

Tang poetry stands at the summit of Chinese literature. Li Bai — the "Immortal Poet" — wrote with romantic abandon; his verse on moonlight and wine remains instantly recognisable after 1,300 years. Du Fu, his contemporary and counterpart, chronicled the devastation of civil war with unflinching realism and is revered as the "Sage of Poetry." Bai Juyi wrote for the common reader, insisting his poems be intelligible to village women. The *Complete Tang Poems* anthologises over 48,900 poems by more than 2,200 authors.

## Key Figures

- **Emperor Taizong (Li Shimin)** r. 626–649 — architect of the Zhenguan era; patron of the arts and vigorous administrator
- **Empress Wu Zetian** — the only woman in Chinese history to rule as emperor in her own name; her reign was marked by capable governance and expanded civil examination recruitment
- **Li Bai** — the Poet Immortal, whose romantic verse defined the Tang spirit
- **Du Fu** — the Poet Sage, whose realist verse documented society before and after the An-Shi Rebellion
- **Xuanzang** — Buddhist monk who journeyed to India and returned with scriptures, inspiring the later novel *Journey to the West*

## The An-Shi Rebellion and Decline

In 755, the general An Lushan launched a devastating rebellion that lasted eight years and killed an estimated 36 million people — one of the deadliest conflicts in premodern history. The Tang court never fully recovered its centralised authority. Regional military governors (*jiedushi*) became semi-independent warlords. Eunuch factions and bureaucratic cliques drained the late Tang of its last vitality. In 907, the warlord Zhu Wen deposed the last Tang emperor, ending nearly three centuries of rule.

## Legacy

The Tang Dynasty's reach extended far beyond its borders. Japan's Nara period was consciously modelled on Tang institutions, architecture, and writing. The Silla kingdom of Korea sent hundreds of students to study in Chang'an. Across Asia, "Tang" became synonymous with Chinese civilisation — a reputation preserved today in the Cantonese word for Chinatown: *Tong yan gaai* (唐人街), "the street of Tang people."
